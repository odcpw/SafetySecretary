# Safety Secretary Early Access Launch Plan

Date: 2026-06-19

## Scope

Safety Secretary should be marketed as a focused, self-hostable incident
investigation coach, not as a broad EHS platform. The useful wedge is simple:
a supervisor or safety practitioner can talk through an incident, the coach asks
better follow-up questions, and the app turns the conversation into structured
facts, causes, actions, and exports.

Switzerland is excluded from outreach, design-partner recruiting, events,
regulator targeting, professional-society targeting, and company targeting for
conflict-of-interest reasons.

Target regions:

- UK and Ireland
- EU outside Switzerland
- North America
- Australia and New Zealand
- Online OSH, HOP, self-hosting, and open-source communities

## Thesis

Win the practitioners who still run incident investigations by hand. Do not try
to win the broad EHS-suite category first.

The strongest growth engine is:

1. Recruit a small group of design partners, especially independent incident
   investigation consultants and trainers.
2. Have them critique one strong synthetic case or run one private sanitized
   case.
3. Convert the feedback into product fixes, quality-ledger notes, and better
   cases.
4. Publish only synthetic cases or explicitly approved sanitized cases.
5. Reuse each case as proof, demo material, SEO content, and an evaluation
   fixture.

The public sanitized case library is the center of the strategy. It is the
marketing proof, the trust artifact, the quality flywheel, and the best way to
show that the tool understands investigation work rather than only producing AI
copy.

## Positioning

Use this language:

- "Self-hostable incident investigation coach"
- "Drafts, asks, structures, and helps capture investigation work"
- "Human acceptance required before suggestions enter the record"
- "Separates actual outcome from credible worst realistic potential outcome"
- "Built for better learning, not blame"
- "Open source under MIT; self-hostable; bring your own model/API key"

Avoid this language:

- "AI finds the root cause"
- "Automated investigation"
- "Compliance guaranteed"
- "Regulator approved"
- "Upload real incident photos to try it"
- "Zero incidents"
- Any legal-assurance or professional-judgment replacement claim

## Priority Plays

### 1. Public Sanitized Case Library

Why it matters: examples beat claims in safety. A good case shows the tool can
handle facts, timelines, actual vs potential severity, weak-cause traps, cause
dependencies, and action quality.

First cases:

- Forklift/pedestrian near miss
- Machine guarding or maintenance pinch injury
- Slip/trip/fall or housekeeping incident
- Lockout/tagout confusion during maintenance
- Chemical splash or PPE/SDS mismatch

Each case should include:

- Plain-language incident story
- Timeline
- Facts known at intake vs discovered later
- Actual outcome and credible worst realistic outcome
- Cause tree or 5 Whys/fishbone view
- Weak causes rejected by the coach
- Accepted actions with owner/type/date shape
- Export screenshots or generated report artifact
- "What the coach got wrong or a human changed"
- Provenance: synthetic, public-domain, or explicitly approved sanitized case

First two weeks:

- Build one flagship forklift near-miss case.
- Run it through the current app.
- Publish the case with the export and a short quality note.

Success metric:

- Three strong public cases in 30 days.
- Ten public synthetic or approved sanitized cases in 90 days.

### 2. Design-Partner Investigation Cohort

Why it matters: the product needs practitioner truth before broad launch.

Recruit 6-8 design partners:

- Independent OSH/RCA consultants
- Incident investigation trainers
- HOP/Learning Teams practitioners
- EHS or operations managers in 50-500 person companies
- Safety educators using fictional training scenarios

The ask:

- Review one flagship synthetic case, or
- Run one private sanitized case, or
- Use the tool in a training scenario and critique the output

First two weeks:

- Create a one-page invitation.
- Contact 20 high-fit people or organizations using public contact forms or
  direct messages only.
- Offer two paths: "critique the case" or "run your sanitized case privately."

Success metric:

- Six design partners recruited.
- Four complete a full investigation workflow.
- Three exports judged usable with minor edits.
- Ten concrete coach-quality defects found and triaged.

### 3. Consultant And Trainer Channel

Why it matters: one consultant can expose the tool to many client investigations
and can produce dense feedback fast.

Best-fit personas:

- Solo or small-firm incident investigation facilitator
- RCA/ICAM/TapRooT-style trainer
- HOP/Learning Teams facilitator
- ISO 45001 or safety-management consultant who handles investigations
- Training provider that needs better investigation exercises

Initial targets to research:

- UK/Ireland: OSHCR-listed consultants, IOSH/IIRSM/RoSPA training providers,
  Fiontar Group, EHS International, Safetech, ASM Group, USC, DuoDynamic Safety,
  LRB Consulting, Safetywise, EHSC
- North America: ASSP practice communities, BCSP credentialed professionals,
  CCOHS guidance readers, Cobalt Safety, Calgary Safety Consultants, Safety
  First BC, Manufacturing Safety Alliance BC
- Australia/NZ: AIHS, NZISM, HASANZ, BWC Safety, Work Safety Hub, Spire Safety,
  HSE Direct, Your Safety Partners, Australian Industry Group WHS consulting
- EU outside Switzerland: ENSHPO-linked national bodies, A+A and Preventica
  networks, Germany/Austria/France/Benelux/Nordics safety consultants and
  training providers

First two weeks:

- Write a consultant-specific page or email section: "Use this as a workshop
  accelerator and investigation drafting aid, not as a replacement for your
  judgment."
- Recruit three consultants for a private walkthrough.

Success metric:

- Three active consultant design partners each run at least one case within six
  weeks.

### 4. Public Quality Ledger

Why it matters: AI in safety is a trust problem first. A quality ledger turns
early weaknesses into explicit, reviewable learning.

Include:

- What the coach is good at
- Known failure modes
- What changed because of design-partner feedback
- How human approval works
- How to report a bad investigation transcript safely
- Links from each public case to the quality notes it produced

Quality criteria:

- Did the coach separate actual harm from credible worst realistic outcome?
- Did it ask useful follow-up questions without becoming a questionnaire?
- Did it avoid blame language?
- Did it push past weak causes?
- Did it propose concrete measures with owner/action/date shape?
- Did the export match the accepted record?

First two weeks:

- Publish a short ledger page with the first flagship case.

Success metric:

- Every public case creates at least one quality note or eval fixture.

### 5. HOP, Safety Differently, And Learning Teams Critique

Why it matters: methodology-forward safety communities are the right audience
for cause trees, weak-cause rejection, actual vs potential severity, and
learning-not-blaming language.

Do not pitch "AI for safety." Ask for critique of a specific case.

Example post:

> I built an early MIT-licensed, self-hostable incident-investigation coach. It
> is not a full EHS suite and it does not make safety decisions. It drafts a
> structured investigation record from a conversation, with human acceptance
> required for suggestions. Here is a sanitized forklift near-miss case. Where
> is the investigation methodologically weak, blame-prone, legally risky, or
> practically useful?

People and ecosystems to research carefully:

- Todd Conklin / PreAccident Investigation / HOP Hub
- Sidney Dekker / Safety Differently
- Andrea Baker / Learning Teams and HOP content
- Rosa Carrillo / relationship-centered safety leadership
- Brent Sutton / HOP and Learning Teams podcast ecosystem

These are learning and credibility ecosystems, not endorsement claims.

Success metric:

- One serious community critique thread.
- At least five high-quality objections converted into case or quality-ledger
  improvements.

### 6. Open-Source Trust Pack

Why it matters: open source helps only if the first ten minutes are clean.
Otherwise it attracts noise and burns credibility.

Before broad launch, have:

- Strong README
- Screenshots or short demo video
- One-command local bootstrap where feasible
- Clear privacy/self-hosting explanation
- Demo data
- License visible
- Support/donation path
- Clear "human acceptance required" boundary
- Clear contribution posture: issues and critique welcome; direct PR merging is
  not the governance model

Launch surfaces after proof assets are ready:

- Hacker News / Show HN
- r/selfhosted
- r/SafetyProfessionals or similar communities with case-first framing
- GitHub topics
- Awesome Selfhosted candidate listing
- AlternativeTo or similar directories

Success metric:

- Five self-host deployments or serious install attempts.
- Two useful external issues that identify real setup or investigation-quality
  problems.

### 7. Methodology Playbook

Why it matters: safety practitioners search for practical investigation method
guidance. A serious guide can rank, earn links, and prove the app is built from
real investigation thinking.

Topics:

- 5 Whys vs cause tree vs fishbone/Ishikawa
- Weak-cause traps
- Actual outcome vs credible worst realistic potential outcome
- Cause dependencies and "therefore" logic
- Corrective actions that are concrete enough to track
- Why the tool asks follow-up questions instead of filling a static form

First two weeks:

- Draft one pillar page and link it to the flagship forklift case.

Success metric:

- Two qualified inbound conversations citing the guide or case.

### 8. Vertical Micro-Playbooks

Why it matters: generic EHS messaging is crowded. Narrow pages make the product
recognizable to the person doing the work.

Start with three:

- Warehousing and logistics: forklift/pedestrian, loading dock, traffic
  segregation
- Small manufacturing and metal fabrication: guarding, maintenance, hand
  injuries, shift handover
- Food processing and packaging: line jams, cleaning, lockout, chemical/PPE
  mismatch

Each playbook:

- One realistic incident
- What Safety Secretary captures
- Common shallow causes to challenge
- Example actions
- Export screenshot
- Design-partner invitation

## 90-Day Sequence

### Weeks 1-3: Build The Proof Engine

- Publish the flagship forklift case.
- Add two more synthetic cases.
- Add a redaction checklist.
- Add the first quality-ledger entry.
- Tighten README/self-host/demo instructions.

### Weeks 3-6: Recruit Design Partners

- Contact 20 consultants/trainers.
- Contact 10 EHS or operations managers in target sectors.
- Post one careful HOP/safety-community critique request.
- Track objections in a simple table: persona, channel, objection, case offered,
  follow-up, outcome.

### Weeks 6-12: Expand What Works

- If consultants respond, create a workshop/training companion page.
- If self-hosted users respond, improve setup docs and demo data.
- If safety communities respond, publish case two and ask a narrower critique
  question.
- If no one responds, improve the flagship case before broadening outreach.

Defer:

- Product Hunt
- Paid ads
- Enterprise EHS suite comparisons as a main campaign
- Conferences without pre-booked meetings
- Insurer/loss-control channel
- Academic partnerships

## Outreach Templates

### Consultant Or Trainer

Subject: critique request - open-source incident investigation coach

I am building Safety Secretary, an MIT-licensed self-hostable tool that helps a
manager investigate an incident by talking it through. It asks follow-up
questions, fills a structured record, and exports a report. It is early and
focused only on incident investigation.

I am looking for incident-investigation trainers or consultants willing to
critique one sanitized case. The useful feedback is blunt: where does the coach
ask the wrong thing, accept a shallow cause, create weak corrective actions, or
produce paperwork you would not use with a client?

If it is useful, I can also help set it up for a private workshop scenario. No
real client data needs to be shared.

### EHS Or Operations Manager

Subject: design-partner request for incident investigation tool

I am looking for a few design partners for Safety Secretary, an open-source
self-hostable incident-investigation coach. The narrow use case is: a supervisor
describes what happened in normal words; the tool asks follow-ups, structures
the timeline/causes/actions, and produces a report.

The design-partner ask is one incident or realistic near miss, preferably
sanitized. You keep your data. Anything public would require explicit approval
and further anonymization.

In return, I will help you run it, listen to what fails, and prioritize fixes
that make the workflow useful for real investigations rather than AI demos.
